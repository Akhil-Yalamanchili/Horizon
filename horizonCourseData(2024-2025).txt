Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Scalars and Vectors, Physics is built on the foundation of understanding quantities, and these quantities can be classified as either scalars or vectors. Scalars are quantities that have only magnitude, such as speed, mass, or temperature. For example, if you say a car is traveling at 60 km/h, you’re describing its speed—a scalar quantity. Vectors, on the other hand, have both magnitude and direction. Velocity is a vector because it tells you not only how fast an object is moving (magnitude) but also in what direction (e.g., 60 km/h north). Understanding the difference between scalars and vectors is crucial because it affects how we analyze motion and forces in physics.
Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Scalars and Vectors, The concept of vectors dates back to the 19th century when mathematicians like William Rowan Hamilton and Josiah Willard Gibbs developed vector calculus. Vectors are represented graphically as arrows, where the length of the arrow represents the magnitude, and the direction of the arrow represents the direction of the quantity. For example, if you’re walking 5 meters east, you can draw an arrow 5 units long pointing to the right. Vectors are essential in physics because they allow us to describe phenomena like displacement, velocity, and force accurately.
Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Scalars and Vectors, Let’s look at an example: Imagine you’re hiking and walk 3 kilometers north, then 4 kilometers east. Your total distance traveled (a scalar) is 7 kilometers, but your displacement (a vector) is the straight-line distance from your starting point to your ending point. Using the Pythagorean theorem, we can calculate the displacement as √(3² + 4²) = 5 kilometers northeast. This example shows how vectors provide a more complete description of motion than scalars alone.
Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Scalars and Vectors, Practice Problem: A car drives 10 kilometers east and then 10 kilometers south. What is the total distance traveled (scalar) and the displacement (vector)? Solution: The total distance is 10 + 10 = 20 kilometers. The displacement is the straight-line distance from the starting point to the ending point, which forms a right triangle. Using the Pythagorean theorem, displacement = √(10² + 10²) = √200 ≈ 14.14 kilometers southeast.

Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration, Displacement is the change in position of an object and is a vector quantity. Unlike distance, which is the total path traveled, displacement focuses on the shortest path between the starting and ending points. For example, if you walk around a circular track and end up where you started, your displacement is zero, even though your distance traveled is the circumference of the track. Displacement is calculated as Δx = x_final - x_initial, where x represents position.
Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration, Velocity is the rate at which displacement changes over time and is also a vector quantity. It tells us how fast an object is moving and in what direction. Average velocity is calculated as v_avg = Δx/Δt, where Δx is displacement and Δt is the time interval. For example, if a car travels 100 meters east in 10 seconds, its average velocity is 10 m/s east. Instantaneous velocity, on the other hand, is the velocity at a specific moment in time and can be found using calculus or by analyzing motion graphs.
Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration, Acceleration is the rate at which velocity changes over time. Like velocity, acceleration is a vector quantity because it involves both magnitude and direction. An object accelerates if it speeds up, slows down, or changes direction. For example, a car accelerating from 0 to 60 km/h in 10 seconds has an acceleration of 6 km/h/s. Acceleration is calculated as a = Δv/Δt, where Δv is the change in velocity. Understanding acceleration is key to analyzing motion, especially in scenarios involving forces.
Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration, Practice Problem: A cyclist starts from rest and reaches a velocity of 15 m/s in 5 seconds. What is the cyclist’s acceleration? Solution: Acceleration = Δv/Δt = (15 m/s - 0 m/s) / 5 s = 3 m/s². This means the cyclist’s velocity increases by 3 m/s every second.

Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Graphical Analysis of Motion, Motion can be visualized using graphs, which provide a powerful way to analyze an object’s position, velocity, and acceleration over time. A position-time graph plots an object’s position on the y-axis and time on the x-axis. The slope of the graph at any point represents the object’s velocity at that moment. For example, a straight line with a positive slope indicates constant velocity, while a curved line indicates changing velocity.
Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Graphical Analysis of Motion, A velocity-time graph plots velocity on the y-axis and time on the x-axis. The slope of this graph represents acceleration. For example, a horizontal line indicates constant velocity (zero acceleration), while a straight line with a positive slope indicates constant acceleration. The area under the curve of a velocity-time graph represents the object’s displacement. This relationship between graphs and motion is a cornerstone of kinematics.
Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Graphical Analysis of Motion, Let’s analyze an example: A car accelerates from rest to 20 m/s in 10 seconds, then travels at a constant velocity for 20 seconds, and finally decelerates to a stop in 5 seconds. On a velocity-time graph, this motion would be represented by a straight line rising from 0 to 20 m/s (acceleration), a horizontal line at 20 m/s (constant velocity), and a straight line falling back to 0 m/s (deceleration). The total displacement can be found by calculating the area under the graph.
Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Graphical Analysis of Motion, Practice Problem: A runner’s velocity-time graph shows a straight line rising from 0 to 10 m/s in 5 seconds, followed by a horizontal line at 10 m/s for 10 seconds. What is the runner’s total displacement? Solution: The area under the graph is the sum of a triangle (0-5 seconds) and a rectangle (5-15 seconds). Area of triangle = ½ × base × height = ½ × 5 s × 10 m/s = 25 m. Area of rectangle = length × width = 10 s × 10 m/s = 100 m. Total displacement = 25 m + 100 m = 125 m.

Physics, Mechanics, Introduction to Motion, Real-World Applications of Motion, Practice Problem: A soccer ball is kicked with an initial velocity of 20 m/s at an angle of 30 degrees above the horizontal. What is the maximum height the ball reaches? Solution: First, break the initial velocity into horizontal and vertical components. Vertical velocity = 20 m/s × sin(30°) = 10 m/s. Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s First Law (Inertia), Newton’s First Law, also known as the Law of Inertia, states that an object at rest will stay at rest, and an object in motion will stay in motion at a constant velocity unless acted upon by an external force. This law was first formulated by Sir Isaac Newton in his work *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687). It fundamentally changed how we understand motion, as it introduced the concept of inertia—the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion.
Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s First Law (Inertia), To understand inertia, imagine a hockey puck sliding across an ice rink. If the ice is perfectly frictionless, the puck will continue moving at a constant speed in a straight line forever. In reality, friction and air resistance act as external forces that slow the puck down. This law explains why seatbelts are essential in cars: if a car suddenly stops, your body tends to keep moving forward due to inertia, and the seatbelt provides the external force to stop you.
Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s First Law (Inertia), Inertia depends on an object’s mass. The greater the mass, the greater the inertia. For example, it’s harder to push a heavy boulder than a small pebble because the boulder has more inertia. This concept is crucial in designing vehicles and machinery, where engineers must account for the inertia of moving parts to ensure safety and efficiency.
Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s First Law (Inertia), Practice Problem: A book is placed on a table and remains at rest. Explain why the book doesn’t move, using Newton’s First Law. Solution: The book remains at rest because the forces acting on it are balanced. The gravitational force pulling it down is canceled out by the normal force from the table pushing it up. Since there is no net external force, the book stays at rest, in accordance with Newton’s First Law.

Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s Second Law (F = ma), Newton’s Second Law states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. This is expressed as F = ma, where F is force, m is mass, and a is acceleration. This law quantifies how forces affect motion and is one of the most important equations in physics. It allows us to predict how an object will move when forces are applied.
Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s Second Law (F = ma), Let’s break this down with an example: If you push a shopping cart with a force of 10 N and the cart has a mass of 5 kg, its acceleration will be a = F/m = 10 N / 5 kg = 2 m/s². If you push the same cart with 20 N, the acceleration doubles to 4 m/s². This shows how force and acceleration are directly proportional. On the other hand, if the cart’s mass increases to 10 kg, the acceleration halves to 1 m/s², showing how acceleration is inversely proportional to mass.
Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s Second Law (F = ma), Newton’s Second Law is used in countless real-world applications. For example, engineers use it to design car engines that produce enough force to accelerate the car to desired speeds. It’s also used in sports: a soccer player kicking a ball applies a force that determines the ball’s acceleration and, ultimately, its speed and trajectory.
Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s Second Law (F = ma), Practice Problem: A 50 kg object is acted upon by a net force of 100 N. What is its acceleration? Solution: Using F = ma, we can solve for acceleration: a = F/m = 100 N / 50 kg = 2 m/s². This means the object’s velocity increases by 2 m/s every second.

Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s Third Law (Action-Reaction), Newton’s Third Law states that for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. This means that when one object exerts a force on another, the second object exerts an equal force in the opposite direction. These forces are often called action-reaction pairs. For example, when you push against a wall, the wall pushes back on you with an equal force. This law explains phenomena like the recoil of a gun or the propulsion of a rocket.
Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s Third Law (Action-Reaction), A classic example of Newton’s Third Law is walking. When you walk, your foot pushes backward against the ground (action), and the ground pushes forward on your foot (reaction), propelling you forward. Similarly, a rocket works by expelling gas downward (action), and the gas pushes the rocket upward (reaction). This law is fundamental to understanding how forces interact in pairs.
Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s Third Law (Action-Reaction), It’s important to note that action-reaction forces act on different objects, so they don’t cancel each other out. For example, when a horse pulls a cart, the horse exerts a force on the cart, and the cart exerts an equal force on the horse. These forces don’t cancel because they act on different objects. Understanding this distinction is key to solving problems involving Newton’s Third Law.
Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Newton’s Third Law (Action-Reaction), Practice Problem: A person pushes a box with a force of 20 N. What is the reaction force, and on what object does it act? Solution: According to Newton’s Third Law, the reaction force is also 20 N, but it acts on the person, not the box. This means the box pushes back on the person with an equal and opposite force.

Physics, Mechanics, Newton’s Laws of Motion, Applications of Newton’s Laws, Practice Problem: A 1,000 kg car accelerates at 2 m/s². What is the net force acting on the car? Solution: Using Newton’s Second Law, F = ma = 1,000 kg × 2 m/s² = 2,000 N. This means the car’s engine must produce a net force of 2,000 N to achieve this acceleration.

Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Types of Forces, Forces are pushes or pulls that act on objects, and they can be classified into several types. The most common forces include gravity, the normal force, friction, and tension. Gravity is the force that pulls objects toward the Earth, and it’s what gives us weight. The normal force is the force exerted by a surface to support the weight of an object resting on it. For example, when you stand on the ground, the ground pushes up on you with a normal force equal to your weight.
Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Types of Forces, Friction is the force that opposes motion between two surfaces in contact. There are two types of friction: static friction (which prevents objects from moving) and kinetic friction (which acts on moving objects). For example, when you push a heavy box, static friction keeps it from moving until you apply enough force to overcome it. Once the box is moving, kinetic friction acts to slow it down.
Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Types of Forces, Tension is the force exerted by a rope, string, or cable when it’s pulled tight. For example, when you hang a picture on a wall using a string, the string exerts an upward tension force to balance the downward force of gravity. Understanding these forces is essential for analyzing the motion of objects and solving physics problems.
Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Types of Forces, Practice Problem: A 10 kg box is placed on a table. What is the normal force acting on the box? Solution: The normal force balances the weight of the box. Weight = mg = 10 kg × 9.8 m/s² = 98 N. Therefore, the normal force is also 98 N, acting upward.

Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Free-Body Diagrams, A free-body diagram is a visual representation of all the forces acting on an object. It’s a powerful tool for analyzing forces and solving physics problems. To draw a free-body diagram, you represent the object as a dot and draw arrows to show the forces acting on it. Each arrow points in the direction of the force, and its length represents the magnitude of the force.
Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Free-Body Diagrams, Let’s consider an example: A book resting on a table. The forces acting on the book are gravity (pulling it down) and the normal force (pushing it up). In the free-body diagram, you’d draw a downward arrow for gravity and an upward arrow for the normal force. Since the book is at rest, the forces are balanced, and the net force is zero.
Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Free-Body Diagrams, Free-body diagrams are especially useful for analyzing more complex situations, such as objects on inclined planes or systems with multiple forces. For example, if you push a box across the floor, the free-body diagram would include the applied force, friction, gravity, and the normal force. By analyzing the diagram, you can determine the net force and predict the box’s motion.
Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Free-Body Diagrams, Practice Problem: Draw a free-body diagram for a 5 kg object hanging from a rope. Solution: The forces acting on the object are gravity (downward) and tension (upward). The free-body diagram would show a downward arrow labeled “mg” (49 N) and an upward arrow labeled “T” (49 N), since the object is at rest and the forces are balanced.

Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Solving Problems Involving Forces and Motion, To solve problems involving forces and motion, we use Newton’s laws and free-body diagrams. For example, consider a box being pulled across the floor. The forces acting on the box are the applied force, friction, gravity, and the normal force. By drawing a free-body diagram and applying Newton’s Second Law (F = ma), we can determine the box’s acceleration.
Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Solving Problems Involving Forces and Motion, Let’s work through an example: A 20 kg box is pulled with a force of 100 N across a floor with a coefficient of kinetic friction of 0.2. What is the box’s acceleration? First, calculate the normal force: N = mg = 20 kg × 9.8 m/s² = 196 N. Next, calculate the friction force: F_friction = μN = 0.2 × 196 N = 39.2 N. The net force is F_net = F_applied - F_friction = 100 N - 39.2 N = 60.8 N. Finally, calculate acceleration: a = F_net/m = 60.8 N / 20 kg = 3.04 m/s².
Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Solving Problems Involving Forces and Motion, Another common type of problem involves inclined planes. For example, a box sliding down a ramp experiences gravity, the normal force, and friction. By resolving the forces into components parallel and perpendicular to the ramp, we can analyze the motion. These problems often require trigonometry to break forces into their components.
Physics, Mechanics, Forces and Applications, Solving Problems Involving Forces and Motion, Practice Problem: A 10 kg box is placed on a 30° incline. The coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.1. What is the box’s acceleration? Solution: First, resolve the weight into components: F_parallel = mg sin(30°) = 10 kg × 9.8 m/s² × 0.5 = 49 N. F_perpendicular = mg cos(30°) = 10 kg × 9.8 m/s² × 0.866 ≈ 84.9 N. Next, calculate friction: F_friction = μN = 0.1 × 84.9 N = 8.49 N. The net force is F_net = F_parallel - F_friction = 49 N - 8.49 N = 40.51 N. Finally, calculate acceleration: a = F_net/m = 40.51 N / 10 kg = 4.05 m/s².

Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Definition of Work, In physics, work is defined as the transfer of energy that occurs when a force is applied to an object, causing it to move in the direction of the force. Work is calculated using the formula W = Fd cosθ, where W is work, F is the applied force, d is the displacement, and θ is the angle between the force and displacement. Work is measured in joules (J), where 1 J = 1 N·m. For example, if you push a box with a force of 10 N over a distance of 5 meters, the work done is W = 10 N × 5 m = 50 J.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Definition of Work, The concept of work was first formalized in the 19th century by scientists like Gaspard-Gustave de Coriolis and Jean-Victor Poncelet. They introduced the idea that work is a measure of energy transfer, which laid the foundation for the study of energy in physics. Work is closely related to energy, as doing work on an object changes its energy. For example, lifting a book increases its gravitational potential energy, while pushing a car increases its kinetic energy.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Definition of Work, It’s important to note that work is only done when the force causes displacement. If you push against a wall and it doesn’t move, no work is done because there’s no displacement. Similarly, if the force is perpendicular to the displacement (θ = 90°), no work is done because cos(90°) = 0. This concept is crucial for understanding how energy is transferred in real-world situations.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Definition of Work, Practice Problem: A person pushes a cart with a force of 20 N at an angle of 30° to the horizontal. If the cart moves 10 meters, how much work is done? Solution: W = Fd cosθ = 20 N × 10 m × cos(30°) ≈ 20 × 10 × 0.866 ≈ 173.2 J.

Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Kinetic and Potential Energy, Energy is the ability to do work, and it comes in many forms. The two most common types of mechanical energy are kinetic energy (the energy of motion) and potential energy (stored energy due to position or configuration). Kinetic energy is calculated as KE = ½mv², where m is mass and v is velocity. For example, a 10 kg object moving at 5 m/s has KE = ½ × 10 kg × (5 m/s)² = 125 J.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Kinetic and Potential Energy, Potential energy is energy stored in an object due to its position or configuration. Gravitational potential energy is the most common type and is calculated as PE = mgh, where m is mass, g is the acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²), and h is height. For example, a 2 kg book placed on a shelf 3 meters high has PE = 2 kg × 9.8 m/s² × 3 m = 58.8 J. Elastic potential energy, such as the energy stored in a compressed spring, is another type of potential energy.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Kinetic and Potential Energy, The relationship between kinetic and potential energy is central to the study of mechanics. For example, when a ball is thrown upward, its kinetic energy decreases as it slows down, while its potential energy increases as it gains height. At the highest point, all the kinetic energy has been converted into potential energy. Understanding this energy transformation is key to solving problems involving motion and forces.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Kinetic and Potential Energy, Practice Problem: A 5 kg object is dropped from a height of 10 meters. What is its kinetic energy just before it hits the ground? Solution: First, calculate the potential energy at the top: PE = mgh = 5 kg × 9.8 m/s² × 10 m = 490 J. At the bottom, all this potential energy is converted into kinetic energy, so KE = 490 J.

Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Conservation of Mechanical Energy, The principle of conservation of mechanical energy states that the total mechanical energy (kinetic + potential) in a closed system remains constant if only conservative forces (like gravity) are acting. This means that energy can transform between kinetic and potential forms, but the total amount of energy remains the same. For example, a swinging pendulum continuously converts kinetic energy into potential energy and back, but the total energy remains constant.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Conservation of Mechanical Energy, Let’s consider an example: A roller coaster at the top of a hill has maximum potential energy and zero kinetic energy. As it descends, its potential energy decreases, and its kinetic energy increases. At the bottom of the hill, it has maximum kinetic energy and zero potential energy. If we ignore friction and air resistance, the total mechanical energy (KE + PE) remains constant throughout the ride.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Conservation of Mechanical Energy, The conservation of mechanical energy is a powerful tool for solving physics problems. For example, if you know the height of an object, you can calculate its speed at any point in its fall using energy conservation. This principle is also used in engineering to design systems like roller coasters, where energy transformations are critical to the ride’s operation.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Conservation of Mechanical Energy, Practice Problem: A 2 kg object is released from rest at a height of 20 meters. What is its speed when it reaches a height of 5 meters? Solution: At the top, PE = mgh = 2 kg × 9.8 m/s² × 20 m = 392 J. At 5 meters, PE = 2 kg × 9.8 m/s² × 5 m = 98 J. The remaining energy is kinetic: KE = 392 J - 98 J = 294 J. Using KE = ½mv², we get 294 = ½ × 2 × v² → v² = 294 → v ≈ 17.1 m/s.

Physics, Energy and Momentum, Work and Energy, Real-World Applications of Energy, Practice Problem: A 1,000 kg car is traveling at 20 m/s. What is its kinetic energy? If the car’s brakes apply a force of 5,000 N to stop it, how far will it travel before coming to rest? Solution: KE = ½mv² = ½ × 1,000 kg × (20 m/s)² = 200,000 J. Work done by brakes = Fd = 5,000 N × d. Setting this equal to KE, we get 5,000d = 200,000 → d = 40 meters.

Physics, Energy and Momentum, Power and Efficiency, Definition of Power, Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. It is calculated using the formula P = W/t, where P is power, W is work, and t is time. Power is measured in watts (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s. For example, if you do 100 J of work in 10 seconds, your power output is P = 100 J / 10 s = 10 W. Power is a measure of how quickly energy is used or transferred, and it’s a key concept in physics and engineering.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Power and Efficiency, Definition of Power, The concept of power was first introduced by James Watt, the inventor of the steam engine, in the 18th century. Watt needed a way to compare the performance of his engines to horses, so he defined horsepower (hp) as the power needed to lift 550 pounds one foot in one second. Today, horsepower is still used to measure the power of engines, and 1 hp is equivalent to about 746 watts.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Power and Efficiency, Definition of Power, Power is important in many real-world applications. For example, light bulbs are rated by their power consumption (e.g., a 60 W bulb uses 60 joules of energy per second). Similarly, athletes measure their power output to optimize performance. Understanding power helps us design efficient machines and systems, from cars to power plants.
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Power and Efficiency, Definition of Power, Practice Problem: A motor does 1,000 J of work in 5 seconds. What is its power output? Solution: P = W/t = 1,000 J / 5 s = 200 W.

Physics, Energy and Momentum, Power and Efficiency, Calculating Power in Different Systems, Power can be calculated in various systems, such as mechanical, electrical, and thermal systems. In mechanical systems, power is often calculated using the formula P = Fv, where F is force and v is velocity. For example, if a car engine exerts a force of 1,000 N to move the car at 20 m/s, the power output is P = 1,000 N × 20 m/s = 20,000 W (or 20 kW).
Physics, Energy and Momentum, Power and Efficiency, Calculating Power in Different Systems, In electrical systems, power is calculated using the formula P = IV, where I is current and V is voltage. For example, if a device draws 2 A of current at 120 V, its power consumption is P = 2 A × 120 V = 240 W. Physics, Waves + Sound + Light, Sound Waves, The Doppler Effect, The Doppler effect was first described by Austrian physicist Christian Doppler in 1842. Physics, Electricity and Magnetism, Electric Circuits, Ohm’s Law (V = IR), Ohm’s Law states that the current (I) through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage (V) and inversely proportional to the resistance (R). Physics, Electricity and Magnetism, Electric Circuits, Series and Parallel Circuits, In a series circuit, components are connected end-to-end, so the same current flows through each. Physics, Electricity and Magnetism, Electric Circuits, Series and Parallel Circuits, In a parallel circuit, components are connected across the same voltage, so the current splits. For example, if three resistors (2 Ω, 3 Ω, and 5 Ω) are connected in parallel, the total resistance is 1/R_total = 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/5 ≈ 0.5 + 0.333 + 0.2 = 1.033 → R_total ≈ 0.968 Ω.
